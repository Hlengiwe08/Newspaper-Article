TEEN INVENTOR LIGHTS UP HIS VILLAGE

SCRAP TO SPARK

In a Limpopo village where candles were a nightly norm, 17-year-old Sipho Nkosi built a mini power grid using salvaged phone parts and car batteries.

“Electricity felt like magic. I wanted to learn the trick,” he said, surrounded by blinking lights in his family's hut.

Sipho’s journey began with a broken radio and curiosity. He watched YouTube videos offline, scavenged junkyards, and taught himself basic circuitry. “I blew up a few wires, but I never stopped,” he said, laughing.

He now powers six homes with homemade solar panels, old batteries, and a basic control box made from melted plastic and wires.

His system supports lightbulbs, a few chargers, and even a small fan. “It’s not Eskom, but we’re not in the dark,” his grandmother said proudly.

Sipho mentors other teens now, running weekend workshops under a tree. A local NGO has offered him a bursary to study electrical engineering next year.

“My dream is to light up the whole province — no more studying by candlelight,” he said.

The project has gained media attention, with engineers and sponsors visiting to support and expand it. “This boy reminds us what innovation really means,” said one donor.
