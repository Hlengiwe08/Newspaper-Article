THE GIRL WHO TAUGHT THE TOWN TO CODE

SIHLE'S STORY

In a small dusty town tucked beyond the hills of Gauteng, one girl's quiet determination sparked a digital revolution. Sihle Ndlovu, only 17, taught herself how to code using a borrowed smartphone and free online tutorials.

Her passion didn’t go unnoticed. Soon, she began teaching neighborhood kids basic HTML and CSS on weekends under a tree next to her home.

From humble beginnings, Sihle’s influence reached far beyond her block. What began as a simple afterschool project transformed into the community’s first coding club. "She made tech feel possible," said one student.

The club, now called *CodeRoots*, has since helped over 40 teens build websites, apps, and even enter provincial hackathons. Parents noticed a change, too — children were staying off the streets and dreaming bigger.

Local businesses soon partnered with the initiative, donating laptops and data bundles. Her story caught the attention of a Johannesburg tech firm that offered her a full scholarship to study computer science next year.

“We had never seen anything like her,” says Mr. Khumalo, her former primary school teacher. “She didn’t just break the mold — she rewrote it.”

With resilience stitched into every line of code, Sihle continues to inspire young girls in rural areas. Her dream is to return after university and start a formal coding academy for township youth.

Her message? “You don’t need to be rich to change your world. Just start where you are, with what you have.”
