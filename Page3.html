THE TEEN WHO GREW A COMMUNITY GARDEN

ROOTS OF CHANGE

At just 16, Lunga Mokoena turned a dusty backyard in Extension 6 into a thriving vegetable garden that now feeds over 20 families every week.

“I just wanted my siblings to eat fresh food,” he said, crouching beside spinach rows. His initiative soon grew into a neighborhood movement.

With no funding, Lunga used recycled buckets, kitchen scraps, and rainwater tanks. Neighbors joined in, bringing seeds and unused tools. “We didn’t wait for help. We became the help,” said a local gogo who volunteers daily.

Schools started sending kids to learn about composting and crop rotation. “It’s life science in action,” joked one teacher. Lunga also started weekend tutorials under a tree, where food meets education.

The garden now grows tomatoes, kale, carrots, and more — all shared freely. It also reduced crime in the area. “When people work together, they protect what they’ve built,” said a local police officer.

Lunga’s dream is to turn the space into a learning hub with Wi-Fi, a greenhouse, and cooking classes for the unemployed. “This soil saved me from the streets. I want it to save others too.”

He’s been nominated for a youth innovation award and was recently invited to speak at an environmental summit in Cape Town.

“It’s not about farming,” he said. “It’s about dignity, purpose, and power in our hands.”
